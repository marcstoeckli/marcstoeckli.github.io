Genetics research matters for economics

17th August 2020 at 11:46am

This is a seedling note. The content is subject to major and frequent revisions (see From seedling to evergreen).

The topic of my dissertation is, broadly, the role of the family (or the household) on the children's success in life. One obvious way how parents have an impact on their children is via genetic inheritance. After all, the child receives half of his or her genetic endowment by the mother, and half by the father. Since there is overwhelming evidence that virtually all behavior traits are at least partly hertiable, the channel of genetic inheritance is tremendously important for economics research (Heritability does not imply determinism; Gene-environment interactions are the holy grail for social scientists). In fact, there is already a name for the field: genoeconomics.

Equality of opportunity

Roemer (1998) distinguished between effort and circumstances as two sources of inequality. The former refers to factors that individuals have control over (e.g. labor supply decisions), whereas the latter cannot be influenced or changed (gender, race, family background, etc.). Inequality in outcomes that are due to individuals' circumstances are deemed unfair and ought to be rectified (think of the gender pay gap). Naturally, this begs the question of the role of genetics for equality of opportunity, an archetypical circumstance.
