Genetics research matters for economics

3rd September 2020 at 8:26am

This is a seedling note. The content is subject to major and frequent revisions (see From seedling to evergreen).

The topic of my dissertation is, broadly, the role of the family (or the household) on children's success in life. One obvious way how parents have an impact on their children is via genetic inheritance. After all, every child receives half of his or her genetic endowment by the mother, and half by the father.

Since there is overwhelming evidence that virtually all behavior traits are at least partly hertiable, the channel of genetic inheritance is tremendously important for economics research. In fact, there is already a name for the field: genoeconomics.

Amongst others, genoeconomics covers topics such as the well-known (and often ill-conceived) notion of nature versus nurture. The discussion about the relative importance of nature (the genetic endowment) compared to nurture (everything else, including family influences) is often subsumed with genetic heritability. However, in my experience talking about this subject, there seem to be an aweful lot of misunderstandings of what heritability is or implies. Importantly, a high degree of heritability does not mean that a phenotype is determined by nature (Heritability does not imply determinism). In fact, heritability is not even constant over time, nor across populations (The heritability of a phenotype is not constant over time).

More interesting – and more relevant – are gene-environment interactions, i.e. how the genetic endowment interacts with the environment (Gene-environment interactions are the holy grail for social scientists).
