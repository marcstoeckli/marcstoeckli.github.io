Gene-environment interactions are the holy grail for social scientists

12th August 2020 at 11:09am

This is a seedling note. The content is subject to major and frequent revisions (see From seedling to evergreen).

The discussion of whether genes or the environment is more important in shaping a trait or outcome is common when discussing genoeconomics, albeit ill-founded.

Two examples

Consider the case of flamingos' pink feathers. There are at least two components that cause the coloring - the genetic architecture on the one side, and organic pigments on the other (representing the environmental component; the pink color is the result of carotenoids in the flamingos' diet of animal and plant plankton). Both components are necessary, yet not sufficient conditions to produce the outcome. That is, the genetic architecture is a strict requirement, but without a diet high in carotenoids, flamingos would nonetheless have white feathers. Likewise, a bird that lacks the relevant genetic architecture would not produce pink feathers when consuming carotenoids. It makes little sense to say that either genes or the environment are more important in producing the color. Rather, both are requirements and interact with each other.

Consider another example. Assume that aggressive behavior is highly heritable, i.e. that the variation in behavior between individuals is largely explained by genetic variation (Heritability does not imply determinism). Whether, say, incarceration (as a result of aggressive behavior) is heritable depends on the environment (The heritability of a phenotype is not constant over time). In a high-crime environment being aggressive might indeed lead to a life behind bars. In contrast, growing up in the "right" environment may yield quite a different result. Aggressive behavior may go hand in hand with assertive behavior, which, given the opportunity, can be helpful in climbing the career ladder.

Finding the holy grail

Gene-environment interactions are the holy grail for social scientists (and for many other researchers). Although there is broad consensus on their existence, it is notoriously difficult to find them.

Amongst other, studies found gene-environment interactions for depressive symptoms (Armin et al. 2020), diabetes (Liu et al. 2015), obesity (Liu et al. 2015, Komulainen et al. 2018)

In countries with low intergenerational mobility, the environment plays a larger role.
